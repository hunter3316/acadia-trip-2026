## Timing and moose behavior in mid-June

Mid-June is a strong moose-photography window in Maine because the animals are tied closely to water, the days are very long, and cows are still accompanied by very young calves. Maine IFW says summer moose spend a great deal of time in **rivers, streams, ponds, lakes, and bogs** because aquatic plants are rich in sodium, but they often feed in **short bouts** and then retreat to the woods to ruminate and seek shade. IFW also says the species is active mostly at **dusk and dawn**, and the species page notes that **cows give birth in May**, meaning that by your dates calves should be present and maternal behavior is a real field consideration. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn35view0Ņłéturn34view0Ņłü

On **June 13**, sunrise is about **4:49 a.m.** and sunset about **8:27 p.m.**; on **June 14ŌĆō15**, sunrise stays about **4:49 a.m.** and sunset moves to **8:28 p.m.** Civil twilight begins around **4:11 a.m.** and runs to roughly **9:05ŌĆō9:06 p.m.** That means your two best daily slots are roughly **4:20ŌĆō7:00 a.m.** and **6:30ŌĆō9:05 p.m.**, with the first hour after sunrise usually giving you the best combination of animal movement, cool air, and clean road conditions. ## Safety, ethics, and weather contingencies

The most important ethical rule is the one Maine IFW states plainly: **if wildlife reacts to your presence, you are too close**. Keep your presence quiet and minimal, avoid slamming car doors, never feed wildlife, and give **extra room to cows with calves**. IFW also notes that if an animal has young, you should keep a **further distance** to avoid stressing the mother. Because your dates fall only a few weeks after calving season, that advice is especially important around all shoreline and bog locations. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn35view0Ņłéturn34view0Ņłü

Road safety matters almost as much as wildlife ethics on this trip. IFWŌĆÖs moose-viewing guidance for dirt roads is explicit: **logging trucks and commercial vehicles have the right of way**, keep to the right, use lights, do not stop in the road, and never block gates or side roads. North Maine Woods regulations use the same principle: **drive slowly and carefully**, and **trucks have the right of way**. Even if you never go deep into North Maine Woods proper, that is the right mindset for Spencer Bay Road beyond Kokadjo. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn35view0Ņłéturn24search1Ņłü

Poor weather does not ruin this trip; it changes which locations make the most sense. **Overcast or misty light is often better for dark-furred moose** than harsh sun, because it lowers contrast and preserves detail around the face and shoulders. In **steady rain or gusty conditions**, Lily Bay becomes your best option because access is easy, the shoreline trail is short, and the park offers multiple formal parking areas; the park also warns that winds on Moosehead Lake can rise suddenly and small craft should stay close to shore, which is another reason to deprioritize any paddling-based backup in bad weather.
